Why Clean Up

Cleaning up beaches is essential for preserving marine ecosystems, as it prevents pollution from harming wildlife and disrupting natural habitats. Removing litter and waste also ensures that beaches remain safe and enjoyable for people, promoting public health and recreation. Additionally, clean beaches support local economies by attracting tourists and enhancing the beauty of coastal areas. Overall, beach cleanups play a crucial role in maintaining the environmental and economic health of coastal communities.

Statistics

Global Water Pollution Impact

Global water pollution impacts over 2 billion people and causes 485,000 deaths annually from diseases like cholera and dysentery. It also disrupts ecosystems, contributing to a 30% decline in freshwater species, underscoring the need for sustainable water management.

Ocean Plastic Pollution

Ocean plastic pollution adds 8 million metric tons of waste yearly, totaling over 150 million metric tons. This affects over 700 marine species, with 90% of seabirds and over half of sea turtles ingesting plastic, causing severe ecological harm.

Agricultural Nutrient Pollution

Agricultural nutrient pollution from nitrogen and phosphorus runoff affects 15 million hectares globally, causing eutrophication and harmful algal blooms. This results in $2.4 billion in annual economic losses in the U.S. due to decreased water quality and damage to fisheries and tourism.

Pollution Facts

  • Even seemingly insignificant amounts of trash, such as a single cigarette butt, can have a significant impact on the ocean. A single cigarette butt can contaminate 8 liters of water after just one hour of soaking in the waves. This contamination can then drift deeper into the ocean, affecting marine life far from the coastline.

  • Participating in beach cleanups can lead to a change in personal behavior, encouraging individuals to reduce their plastic use, recycle more, and avoid using harsh chemicals in their yards that might end up in waterways. This shift in behavior highlights the potential for individual actions to significantly impact the prevention of beach pollution.

  • In 2018, over 1 million volunteers across 22 countries participated in the International Coastal Cleanup, collecting plastic pollution along more than 22,000 miles of coastline. This massive effort underscores the global commitment to addressing ocean pollution and the role of citizen science in gathering data and removing trash from coastlines.

  • The accumulation of trash on beaches and in the ocean, including microplastics from synthetic clothing fibers, poses a significant threat to marine ecosystems. With estimates suggesting that billions of plastic microfibers are present in the ocean per square kilometer, the importance of removing every piece of trash becomes clear. This emphasizes the need for ongoing efforts to clean up existing pollution and prevent future contributions.
